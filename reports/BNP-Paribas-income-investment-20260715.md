## 1. Executive summary

**Analytical judgment — `HOLD – DO NOT ADD`.** BNP Paribas fits **conviction + durable income** better than opportunistic income: its diversified banking franchise, 50% minimum cash-dividend policy and regulatory-capital headroom support the distribution. It is not a yield trap. The constraint is allocation and price, not present dividend safety.

**Verified fact.** For 2025 BNP reported €10.29 EPS and paid €5.16 per share, a 50% cash payout. At 31 March 2026, CET1 was 12.8% against a 10.42% SREP requirement; 1Q26 net income rose 9.0% to €3.217bn, while cost of risk reached 39 bps, close to management's 2026 ceiling of below 40 bps.

**Estimate.** At the supplied €102.32 close, the trailing gross yield is 5.04%, PE is 9.94× and price/book is 0.98×. The three-share holding produces €15.48 annual gross income on the last declared annual dividend, or €10.62 under a simplified 2026 French PFU scenario of 31.4%. The latter is not a forecast of DEGIRO cash receipts.

**Analytical judgment.** The current price offers a fair dividend-led return but little margin of safety for bank, credit-cycle and litigation tail risk. The latest portfolio review already says no immediate trade; BNP is 3.43% of assets and total financial exposure is 21.38%. Reinforcement should wait for both a price at or below €90 and lower portfolio financial concentration.

## 2. Verdict and category

**Verdict: `HOLD – DO NOT ADD`**

- **Possible role:** existing 2–4% satellite/core-income bank position, not a stand-alone defensive bond substitute.
- **Primary reason:** earnings, capital and the stated payout policy presently cover a growing semiannual dividend.
- **Primary risk:** a credit/legal shock can consume capital and make a regulated bank's payout discretionary.
- **Entry condition:** study below €90, only if CET1 remains at least 12.5%, cost of risk remains at or below roughly 40–45 bps, and portfolio finance exposure falls to about 20% or less.
- **Position-size range to study:** 2–4% of portfolio value; 5% is a monitoring ceiling, not a universal recommendation.
- **Reinforcement condition:** price at or below €90 plus unchanged capital/distribution thesis and no adverse Sudan-litigation development.
- **Reduction/exit criteria:** CET1 below 11.5%, cost of risk above 60 bps for two reporting periods, dividend cut not caused solely by a system-wide temporary regulator ban, or weight above 5% without improved diversification.
- **Monitoring:** CET1, distance to MDA restrictions, cost of risk, impaired loans, 2026 interim dividend, legal provisions and Sudan appeal.
- **Confidence:** medium-high on current safety; medium on five-year growth because banks are cyclical and regulated.

## 3. Possible portfolio role

**Analytical judgment.** BNP can provide EUR-denominated, growing bank income and exposure to a diversified European financial franchise. It is less suitable as a defensive income anchor because bank dividends depend on capital ratios, regulator consent, credit costs and market confidence.

**Analytical judgment.** The holding is conviction income rather than opportunistic yield: there is no short-term catalyst/exit structure, and the 5.04% trailing yield is not unusually high against BNP's own recent year-end yield proxy. The sensible role is a maintained small position, not a yield-driven trade.

## 4. Business and source of distributed cash

**Verified fact.** BNP earns net banking income from Commercial, Personal Banking & Services, Corporate & Institutional Banking, and Investment & Protection Services. 1Q26 net banking income was €14.056bn: CPBS €6.852bn, CIB €5.243bn and IPS €1.980bn before corporate-centre effects.

**Analytical judgment.** For a bank, free cash flow and net debt are misleading distribution measures. The relevant chain is recurring net income → retained earnings/CET1 generation → regulatory headroom → board/AGM distribution. Deposits and wholesale debt are operating funding, not industrial-company leverage.

**Verified fact.** BNP's 2026 distribution policy is 60% of earnings, with at least 50% in cash dividends and 10% in buybacks. Buybacks compete with retained capital but are the more flexible component; the dividend is the income component.

## 7. Balance sheet and refinancing

**Verified fact.** At 31 March 2026, CET1 was 12.8%, 238 bps above the 10.42% requirement. The leverage ratio was 4.4%, the liquidity coverage ratio 125%, HQLA €359bn and immediately available liquidity reserve €464bn.

**Verified fact.** BNP reported €18bn CET1 distance and €16bn minimum overall distance to Maximum Distributable Amount restrictions. Total capital was 17.3% versus a 14.60% requirement. These are buffers, not cash available to shareholders.

**Verified fact.** The 2026 regulatory issuance plan was €14bn; about 60% was completed by 1 May 2026. Wholesale medium/long-term funding outstanding was €264bn, with economic maturities spread across years. BNP showed €13bn of senior non-preferred maturities in 2026, €11bn in 2027 and €11bn in 2028.

**Analytical judgment.** Refinancing is manageable in the base case. A market-access freeze would still be material because BNP is a leveraged G-SIB; liquidity reserves and loss-absorbing debt reduce, but do not eliminate, that tail risk.

## 8. Income growth

**Verified fact.** Dividend/share rose from €3.67 in 2021 to €5.16 in 2025 while 2025 EPS increased to €10.29. 1Q26 revenue growth of 8.5% and net-income growth of 9.0% support near-term distributable-earnings momentum.

**Analytical judgment.** A normalized 3–6% dividend growth range is more prudent than extrapolating the 8.89% historical CAGR. Capital requirements, credit losses, share count, legal costs and the split between dividends and buybacks will determine actual growth.

**Verified fact.** A €219m 1Q26 provision for UK motor-finance legal risk reduced group net income by €98m after tax and minorities. This illustrates why accounting/legal charges can interrupt a smooth dividend-growth path even when underlying operations improve.

## 10. Tax and currency

**Verified fact.** The share and distributions are denominated in EUR, matching the portfolio's reference currency. BNP's underlying earnings remain exposed to GBP, USD and other currencies through international operations.

**Verified fact.** France's official 2026 guidance states that the default PFU for dividends comprises 12.8% income tax plus 18.6% social levies, or 31.4% in total. A progressive-scale election is global across relevant investment income and may permit a 40% dividend allowance; its benefit depends on the taxpayer's full situation.

**Data gap.** DEGIRO's actual French advance withholding, social-levy collection and declaration timing were not confirmed. ## 14. Purchase or reinforcement conditions

**Analytical judgment.** Do not add now. Reconsider only when all of the following hold:

- price at or below €90, preferably below €85 for a strong margin of safety;
- CET1 at least 12.5% and clear progress toward 13% in 2027;
- cost of risk no worse than 40–45 bps and no sharp impaired-loan acceleration;
- confirmed interim dividend consistent with at least a 50% cash payout;
- no material adverse Sudan judgment/settlement or unreserved legal charge;
- portfolio finance exposure about 20% or less after the trade.

**Analytical judgment.** Do not buy merely before an ex-dividend date. The price adjustment and 31.4% PFU baseline eliminate any mechanical income advantage.

## 15. Reduction or sale conditions

**Analytical judgment.** Study reduction if any one fundamental condition becomes persistent: CET1 below 11.5%; cost of risk above 60 bps for two periods; capital distribution suspended for bank-specific reasons; an inadequately reserved legal loss that threatens the 2027 capital target; or a structural fall in RoTE below the cost of equity.

**Analytical judgment.** Study valuation-led trimming above €125 unless sustainable RoTE rises above roughly 11–12% and dividend growth justifies the rerating. Also trim/rebalance if BNP exceeds 5% or finance exposure rises materially above the current 21.38%.

**Analytical judgment.** Yield on cost of 7.99% is not a hold reason. The decision must use current value, forward income and alternative uses of capital.
